Complex Numbers

What are complex numbers? To understand this, we must first understand imaginary numbers. Let's start with the basics, the natural numbers. These are the numbers like 1, 2, 3, 4, etc. What is great about these numbers is that we can add them. 2 + 3 = 5, 7 + 5 = 12. This is great! What is especially nice is that any two natural numbers added together create another natural number. This is also the case for multiplication, like 9 ⨯ 8 = 72. The natural numbers are a set (a group) of numbers. The operations of addition and multiplication, if given numbers from this set, do not give values outside the set. This means that addition and multiplication are "closed" under the natural numbers. For an operation to be closed under a set, it must not leave the set by returning a value not in the set.

What if we added subtraction? All of the sudden, the natural numbers are not enough! If we allow subtraction, what will 4 - 6 evaluate to? No such number exists, therefore it is impossible. It makes no sense to ask such a question or to create such an operation. Except, not really. It is possible if we extend our idea of what a number is. How about instead of the natural numbers, we use the integers. This set includes negative numbers, so our problem is solved! Addition, subtraction, and multiplication are closed under the integers. This is perfect. What's that? You want division? Sure! 32 / 4 = 8. Perfect. 7 / 5 = ...? Maybe integers are not enough. Let's add rational numbers! Now we have all the operations we could ever want.

I think you are starting to see a pattern. Whenever we encounter an operation that is not closed under our number system, we can expand our definition of a number. How about we add another operation? Exponents seem useful.

$$ 7^3 = 343 $$

$$ 2^\frac{1}{2} = ? $$

Okay, you got me. Let's add irrational numbers. This would equal the square root of two, and the square root is asking the question "what number times itself equals this?".

$$ \sqrt{-1} = ? $$

This isn't hard. We just need to find a number, that when multiplied by itself, equals negative one. 1 ⨯ 1 = 1. That's not it. -1 ⨯ -1 = 1. Hmm... 2 ⨯ 2 = 4, -2 ⨯ -2 = 4. It seems that no such number exists. Therefore, it is impossible. It makes no sense to ask such a question or to create such an operation. Any number times itself is postive, not negative. Does this sound familiar? Exponents are not closed under the reals (this just means irrationals, rationals, and integers). This means the reals are not the full picture. Finally, we reach the definition of an imaginary number:

$$ i = \sqrt{-1} $$

This feels like cheating. We can't just define an answer to exist! The square root of negative one is nonsense, right? Nope. Numbers are what we define them to be. It made sense to have negative numbers, although you would have a hard time finding one in nature. Mathematicians have defined "nonsense" like negative and imaginary numbers because they are so useful. They fill in "holes" in our system, keeping it closed.

Now we can define complex numbers. Complex numbers are simply numbers with a real and an imaginary component.

$$ z = a+bi $$

$$ c = 3.141 - 5i $$

The complex number z has a real part, a, and an imaginary part, b ⨯ i. The complex number c is an example of a number in this form. Operations such as addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, and exponentiation are well defined for complex numbers.
