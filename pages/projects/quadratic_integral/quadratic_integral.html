The General Form of the Integral of Quadratic Polynomials

In this article I mention concepts you may be unfamiliar with. Click on the links to see my explanation of them!

This project involves a basic understanding of calculus, and unfortunately I do not have an explanation page available due to calculus being such a large topic. With that being said, I found the antiderivative of all square roots quadratic polynomials.

Here is what that means. Quadratic polynomials are polynomials which have a highest degree of 2, and they can be shown by any equation which follows the form

$$ ax^{2} + bx + c $$

where a, b, and c are constants.

Finding the antiderivative (integral) of a polynomial is trivial. This is because they only consist of constants times a variable to an integer power, and each term is summed. It is significantly more difficult to find the integral of the square root of quadratic polynomials. While for some it is trivial (like the square root of x squared), most of these functions create ellipses or hyperbolas. These shapes are quite complicated to integrate, and there has to be one formula for them all.

I was inspired by my calculus 2 class to find this integral. I was learning about trigonometric substitution to find integrals similar to the general form, but much less complex. Trigonometric substitution works by replacing part of the integral with a trigonometric function temporarily to apply useful trigonometric identities to simplify the integral. To use these techniques, I needed to change the form of the quadratic. I needed it in the form

$$ A(x-B)^{2} + C $$

Now I had to convert the original polynomial into this one. This was pretty simple, because the form I needed it in is vertex form. The vertex of a polynomial is at the point (B, C) when following vertex form. The vertex is also where the derivative equals zero, so I did some calculus to find that

$$ A = a $$

$$ B = -\frac{b}{2a} $$

$$ C = -\frac{b^{2}}{4a} + c $$

Now I could start substituting. Well, almost. I will simplify the interior of the square root.

$$ \int{\sqrt{A(x-B)^{2} + C}}\,dx $$

$$ \sqrt{A}\int{\sqrt{(x-B)^{2} + \frac{C}{A}}}\,dx $$

Substitution time! I am using hyperbolic trigonometry because it is better defined (no holes) and it has the trigonometric idendity I need.

$$ \cosh^2{x} - \sinh^2{x} = 1 $$

$$ x - B = \sqrt{\frac{C}{A}}\sinh{\theta} $$

$$ dx = \sqrt{\frac{C}{A}}\cosh{\theta}\,d\theta $$

$$ \sqrt{A}\int{\sqrt{(\sqrt{\frac{C}{A}}\sinh{\theta})^{2} + \frac{C}{A}}}\sqrt{\frac{C}{A}}\cosh{\theta}\,d\theta $$

$$ \sqrt{C}\int{\sqrt{\frac{C}{A}\sinh^2{\theta} + \frac{C}{A}}}\cosh{\theta}\,d\theta $$

$$ \sqrt{C}\sqrt{\frac{C}{A}}\int{\sqrt{\sinh^2{\theta} + 1}}\cosh{\theta}\,d\theta $$

$$ \frac{C}{\sqrt{A}}\int{\sqrt{\cosh^2{\theta}}}\cosh{\theta}\,d\theta $$

$$ \frac{C}{\sqrt{A}}\int{\cosh^2{\theta}}\,d\theta $$

That was a lot of math! But now the integral is significantly more simple. What is nice about hyperbolic trigonometry is that its sine and cosine functions are just exponetial functions added, and these are very easy to integrate and differentiate. Here we can simplify it.

$$ \frac{C}{\sqrt{A}}\int{\cosh^2{\theta}}\,d\theta = \frac{1}{4}\sinh{2\theta} + \frac{1}{2}\theta$$

This is the answer in terms of theta, but we want it in terms of x.

$$ \theta = \sinh^{-1}{(\frac{x - B}{\sqrt{\frac{C}{A}}})}$$

$$ \frac{1}{4}\sinh{2\theta} + \frac{1}{2}\theta = $$

$$ \frac{1}{4}\sinh{(2\sinh^{-1}{(\frac{x - B}{\sqrt{\frac{C}{A}}})})} + \frac{1}{2}(\sinh^{-1}{(\frac{x - B}{\sqrt{\frac{C}{A}}})}) $$

At this point, we are done. We have found the antiderivative in terms of x. But, we can simplify further with the identity

$$ \sinh^{-1}{x} = \ln{(x + \sqrt{x^2+1})} $$

After a lot of algebra, we finally get the simplified answer.

$$ \int{\sqrt{ax^2 + bx + c}}\,dx = $$

$$ \frac{2ax+b}{4a}\sqrt{ax^2 + bx + c} + \frac{4ac - b^2}{8a^2}\ln{(\frac{2ax+b+2\sqrt{a}\sqrt{ax^2 + bx + c}}{\sqrt{4ac-b^2}})}\sqrt{a} $$
